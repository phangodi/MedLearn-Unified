  'Inspiratory Phase (Inspiration): Motor neurons active: Phrenic motor neurons (innervate the diaphragm) and External intercostal motor neurons. Effect: Diaphragm and external intercostals contract → thoracic volume increases → air flows in. Motoneuron activity: Bursting discharge in a ramp-like pattern, gradually increasing during inspiration. Controlled by: Inspiratory neurons in the dorsal respiratory group (DRG) and the pre-Bötzinger complex in the medulla.',
  'Postinspiratory Phase (Early Expiration / Passive Recoil): Motor neurons active: Laryngeal and pharyngeal motor neurons. Effect: Control glottal opening and airway resistance to regulate airflow. Smooth transition from inspiration to expiration. Motoneuron activity: Decrease in phrenic activity; brief activation of airway-related motor neurons. Modulated by: Bötzinger complex (inhibitory neurons) and Pontine Respiratory Group (PRG), particularly the pneumotaxic center, which limits inspiration and facilitates transition into expiration, shaping postinspiratory activity. Note: Prominent during regulated breathing (e.g., sighing, speaking).',
  'Expiratory Phase (Active Expiration, if needed): Motor neurons active: Internal intercostal motor neurons and Abdominal motor neurons. Effect: Actively contract to force air out (during speech, exercise, or coughing). Motoneuron activity: Typically silent during quiet breathing; active during forced expiration. Controlled by: Expiratory neurons in the ventral respiratory group (VRG) of the medulla.'

// **LO-4** (Topic-30)
officialDefinitions: [
  'Dorsal Respiratory Group (DRG): Location: The DRG is situated in the medulla oblongata, specifically within the nucleus tractus solitarius (NTS), which is a critical relay center for sensory signals. Function: The DRG is primarily responsible for generating the basic rhythm of breathing, particularly during quiet, restful breathing (eupnea). It mainly contains neurons that control inspiratory muscles, especially the diaphragm and external intercostal muscles.',
  'DRG Input and Integration: The DRG receives afferent input from various sources: Peripheral Chemoreceptors: Signals from the carotid and aortic bodies via the glossopharyngeal (CN IX) and vagus (CN X) nerves, respectively. These chemoreceptors monitor blood pH, pCO2, and pO2 levels. Lung Stretch Receptors: Via the vagus nerve, providing feedback on lung inflation. Other Sensory Inputs: Mechanoreceptors from the respiratory tract and proprioceptors from the muscles and joints. The DRG integrates these sensory signals to adjust the rhythm and depth of breathing according to metabolic needs.',
  'DRG Output: The DRG sends inspiratory signals to the phrenic nerve, which innervates the diaphragm, and to the intercostal nerves, which innervate the external intercostal muscles.',
  'Ventral Respiratory Group (VRG): Location: The VRG is also located in the medulla oblongata but is more ventrally positioned compared to the DRG. Function: The VRG contains both inspiratory and expiratory neurons. Unlike the DRG, which is active during quiet breathing, the VRG is primarily involved in forced respiration (e.g., during exercise, talking, or coughing). It plays a key role in stimulating both inspiratory and expiratory muscles for active breathing efforts.',
  'VRG Subdivisions: Rostral VRG (Botzinger complex): Contains neurons that are primarily expiratory and help inhibit inspiratory activity during expiration. Intermediate VRG (including the Pre-Bötzinger complex): Contains pacemaker neurons that are essential for generating the rhythmic pattern of breathing. Caudal VRG: Contains expiratory neurons that stimulate muscles such as the internal intercostals and abdominal muscles during active expiration.',
  'VRG Output: Sends signals to accessory inspiratory and expiratory muscles through spinal motor neurons, aiding in increased respiratory effort.',
  'Pre-Bötzinger Complex: Location: Located within the intermediate portion of the VRG in the medulla. Importance: The Pre-Bötzinger complex is crucial for the generation of the respiratory rhythm. It is considered the primary pacemaker site for breathing. This cluster of neurons generates rhythmic bursts of action potentials that initiate the inspiratory phase of breathing.',
  'Pre-Bötzinger Complex Mechanism: The neurons in the Pre-Bötzinger complex exhibit pacemaker-like activity, meaning they can generate spontaneous rhythmic firing. The output from the Pre-Bötzinger complex coordinates with other regions to produce a stable breathing pattern. It interacts with the DRG to ensure a continuous, rhythmic inspiratory signal and regulates the switch between inspiration and expiration.',
  'Pontine Respiratory Group (PRG): Location: Found in the pons. Consists of two major nuclei: Parabrachial nucleus and Kölliker-Fuse nucleus. These nuclei together form what is traditionally called the pneumotaxic center, which modulates respiratory rhythm and transitions between inspiration and expiration.',
  'Parabrachial Nucleus: Function: Modulates the depth and rate of breathing by sending inhibitory signals to medullary respiratory centers (e.g., DRG, VRG). Role: Prevents over-inflation of lungs by shortening inspiration. Aids the switch from inspiration to expiration. Regulation: Responds to sensory inputs like CO₂ and O₂ levels. Ensures adaptable breathing during speech, emotional responses, or metabolic demand. Significance: Fine-tunes breathing based on changing needs (e.g., talking, singing, sighing).',
  'Kölliker-Fuse Nucleus: Function: Works with the parabrachial nucleus to smoothly transition between inspiration and expiration. Role: Refines inspiratory depth and duration. Prevents abrupt transitions or apneustic (prolonged inspiratory) breathing. Significance: Vital for precise respiratory control, such as during speech or voluntary breath-holding. Helps maintain a stable and rhythmic breathing pattern.'
],

// **LO-5** (Topic-30)
officialDefinitions: [
  'Hering-Breuer Inflation Reflex: Function: Prevents over-inflation of the lungs during deep inspiration. Receptors Involved: Slowly adapting stretch receptors (SARs) in the smooth muscle of airways. Stimulus: Lung inflation (increased stretch of bronchi and bronchioles).',
  'Hering-Breuer Reflex Arc: Afferent limb: Vagal afferents (myelinated fibers) from stretch receptors. Integration center: Brainstem (medulla – DRG). Efferent limb: Inhibition of inspiratory motor neurons (e.g., phrenic nerve). Effect: Inhibits inspiration, promoting expiration. Helps regulate tidal volume and prevents lung overexpansion.',
  'Irritant Receptor Reflex: Function: Protects airways from harmful substances and promotes airway clearance. Receptors Involved: Rapidly adapting receptors (RARs) located in the epithelium of the trachea and bronchi. Stimulus: Dust, smoke, cold air, histamine, or mechanical stimulation.',
  'Irritant Receptor Reflex Arc: Afferent limb: Vagal afferents from RARs. Integration center: Brainstem (medulla and pons). Efferent limb: Respiratory motor neurons and autonomic efferents. Effect: Bronchoconstriction, coughing, mucus secretion, and tachypnea (rapid shallow breathing). Temporarily reduces tidal volume and increases respiratory rate.',
  'J-Receptors (Juxtapulmonary-Capillary Receptors): Sensory receptors located in the alveolar walls near the capillaries of the lungs. They are primarily sensitive to changes in the pulmonary interstitial environment, especially in response to events that affect the pulmonary capillaries and alveoli, such as pulmonary congestion, edema, or inflammation. The activation of these receptors leads to specific reflex responses known as J-receptor reflexes or chemoreflexes.',
  'J-Receptor Activation: J-receptors are activated by various pulmonary changes, including: Increased interstitial fluid (edema) in the lungs, commonly due to left-sided heart failure, which leads to congestion in the lungs. Pulmonary capillary engorgement, where there is an increased blood volume in the lung capillaries. Exposure to certain chemicals or inflammatory mediators, such as in lung infections or inflammation.',
  'J-Receptor Signal Transmission and Reflex Arc: Once activated, J-receptors send signals through the vagus nerve to the respiratory centers in the medulla and pons.',
  'J-Receptor Reflex Responses: The stimulation of J-receptors triggers several reflexive responses, collectively known as the J-receptor chemoreflexes: Rapid, shallow breathing: Activation of J-receptors causes a reflex response that leads to shallow, quick breaths. This is thought to protect the lungs by minimizing additional fluid buildup and reducing the workload on the respiratory muscles. Bronchoconstriction: Constriction of the bronchial smooth muscles occurs, which may limit the air volume entering the lungs, potentially as a protective response. Bradycardia and Hypotension: In some cases, stimulation of J-receptors can also trigger a slowing of the heart rate (bradycardia) and a drop in blood pressure (hypotension).',
  'J-Receptor Clinical Relevance: J-receptor reflexes are often seen in conditions involving lung pathology, such as pulmonary edema, acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), or congestive heart failure. This reflex is also implicated in the sensation of dyspnea (shortness of breath), as the rapid, shallow breathing and bronchoconstriction can lead to feelings of respiratory discomfort.'
],

officialDefinitions: [
  'Eupnoea: Eupnoea refers to normal, unlabored breathing, which is the typical, relaxed state of respiration that occurs at rest. It is rhythmic, steady, and meets the body\'s metabolic needs without any discomfort or effort.',
  'Hypopnoea: Hypopnoea is a condition characterized by abnormally shallow or slow breathing. This type of breathing reduces ventilation, leading to decreased oxygen intake and carbon dioxide removal, which may be associated with sleep disorders like sleep apnea.',
  'Hyperpnoea: Hyperpnoea refers to an increased depth and rate of breathing that exceeds the body\'s basic metabolic requirements. Unlike hyperventilation, hyperpnoea is usually a normal response to increased physical activity or exercise to meet the increased oxygen demand and removal of carbon dioxide.',
  'Dyspnoea: Dyspnoea is the medical term for the subjective sensation of difficult or labored breathing, commonly described as "shortness of breath." It is often associated with respiratory or cardiovascular conditions, physical exertion, or psychological states such as anxiety.'

// **LO-7** (Topic-30)
officialDefinitions: [
  'Kussmaul Breathing: Kussmaul breathing is a deep, labored, and rapid breathing pattern. It is typically associated with metabolic acidosis, particularly diabetic ketoacidosis. The body tries to compensate for the acidosis by increasing the rate and depth of breathing to expel more carbon dioxide and reduce the blood\'s acidity. It is considered a type of hyperventilation and is an indicator of severe metabolic imbalance.',
  'Cheyne-Stokes Breathing: Cheyne-Stokes breathing is a cyclical pattern of breathing characterized by a gradual increase in breathing depth and rate, followed by a gradual decrease, resulting in a temporary stop in breathing (apnea). The pattern repeats with each cycle lasting anywhere from 30 seconds to 2 minutes. It is commonly observed in conditions such as congestive heart failure, stroke, traumatic brain injuries, and in some cases during sleep (especially in the elderly). This pattern is thought to be related to delayed feedback from the central nervous system in response to changes in blood CO2 and O2 levels.',
  'Apneustic Breathing (Apneusia): Apneustic breathing is a pattern characterized by a prolonged inspiratory phase followed by an inadequate or insufficient expiratory phase. This type of breathing often leads to gasping or pauses at full inspiration before exhalation begins. It is typically associated with damage to the pons or upper brainstem, which can occur due to stroke, trauma, or other neurological damage.',
  'Gasping: Gasping is a sudden, forceful, and irregular inspiration, often associated with an intense need for air. It is characterized by labored and jerky breathing that is often noisy. Gasping is seen in situations of severe oxygen deprivation or in the agonal state, which occurs before death or during cardiac arrest. It is considered a reflex response in an emergency situation when the body is severely hypoxic and struggling for oxygen.'
