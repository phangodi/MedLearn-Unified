// **LO-2** (Topic-57) CRITICAL
// Describe the luminal mechanisms of Na⁺-reabsorption in the proximal tubulus, thick ascending limb, distal convoluted tubulus, and collecting duct. Which transport is under hormonal control?
officialDefinitions: [
  'Proximal Tubule (reabsorbs ~65–70% of filtered Na⁺): (1) Na⁺-Solute Co-Transport: sodium is reabsorbed along with other solutes like glucose, amino acids, phosphate, and lactate via sodium-solute co-transporters (e.g., SGLT1 and SGLT2). (2) Na⁺-H⁺ Antiporter (NHE3): sodium is exchanged for hydrogen ions (H⁺) via the Na⁺-H⁺ exchanger (NHE3), a key mechanism for sodium reabsorption and acid-base balance. (3) Paracellular Transport: sodium reabsorption occurs through tight junctions (paracellular pathway) driven by electrochemical gradients and solvent drag (water reabsorption pulls sodium along with it).',
  'Thick Ascending Limb of the Loop of Henle (reabsorbs ~20–25% of filtered Na⁺): Na⁺–K⁺–2Cl⁻ Symporter (NKCC2): sodium is co-transported with potassium (K⁺) and chloride (Cl⁻) via the Na⁺–K⁺–2Cl⁻ symporter. This segment is impermeable to water, so sodium reabsorption dilutes the tubular fluid and contributes to the countercurrent multiplication system, which creates a hyperosmolar medulla.',
  'Distal Convoluted Tubule (reabsorbs ~5–7% of filtered Na⁺): Na⁺–Cl⁻ Symporter (NCC): sodium is co-transported with chloride (Cl⁻) via the Na⁺–Cl⁻ symporter (NCC). This segment is important for fine-tuning sodium reabsorption.',
  'Collecting Duct (reabsorbs ~3–5% of filtered Na⁺): Epithelial Sodium Channels (ENaCs): sodium is reabsorbed via ENaCs in the principal cells of the collecting duct. This process is regulated by aldosterone, which increases the number and activity of ENaCs, enhancing sodium reabsorption.',
  'Hormonal control: (1) Aldosterone acts on the collecting duct (principal cells) to increase ENaC expression and activity, enhance sodium reabsorption and potassium secretion, released in response to low blood volume/pressure via the renin-angiotensin-aldosterone system (RAAS). (2) Antidiuretic hormone (ADH) indirectly affects sodium reabsorption by enhancing water reabsorption in the collecting duct, concentrating sodium in the medulla. (3) Natriuretic peptides (e.g., ANP) inhibit sodium reabsorption in the collecting duct and promote natriuresis (sodium excretion).'
],

// **LO-3** (Topic-57)
// Identify the targets of the following diuretics: carbonic anhydrase inhibitors, loop diuretics (furosemide), thiazides, K⁺-sparing diuretics (aldosterone-antagonists).
officialDefinitions: [
  'Carbonic Anhydrase Inhibitors (e.g., Acetazolamide): Target: carbonic anhydrase enzyme in the proximal tubule. Mechanism: inhibits carbonic anhydrase, an enzyme necessary for the reabsorption of bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻) and sodium (Na⁺). Reduces HCO₃⁻ reabsorption, resulting in increased excretion of bicarbonate, sodium, potassium, and water. Effects: leads to alkaline urine and mild diuresis; can cause metabolic acidosis due to loss of bicarbonate.',
  'Loop Diuretics (e.g., Furosemide): Target: Na⁺–K⁺–2Cl⁻ symporter (NKCC2) in the thick ascending limb of the loop of Henle. Mechanism: inhibits the NKCC2 symporter, blocking the reabsorption of sodium, potassium, and chloride. Disrupts the medullary osmotic gradient, impairing water reabsorption in the collecting ducts. Effects: produces powerful diuresis by increasing the excretion of sodium, potassium, calcium, magnesium, and water; reduces plasma volume and decreases blood pressure.',
  'Thiazide Diuretics (e.g., Hydrochlorothiazide): Target: Na⁺–Cl⁻ symporter (NCC) in the distal convoluted tubule. Mechanism: inhibits the Na⁺–Cl⁻ symporter, blocking sodium and chloride reabsorption in the distal tubule. Enhances calcium reabsorption via effects on the sodium-calcium exchanger. Effects: causes moderate diuresis by increasing sodium, chloride, and water excretion; reduces calcium excretion, beneficial for preventing kidney stones.',
  'Potassium-Sparing Diuretics - Aldosterone Antagonists (e.g., Spironolactone, Eplerenone): Target: aldosterone receptor in the collecting duct (principal cells). Mechanism: blocks aldosterone from stimulating the synthesis of epithelial sodium channels (ENaCs) and Na⁺–K⁺ ATPase pumps. Reduces sodium reabsorption and potassium excretion. Effects: produces mild diuresis while conserving potassium; reduces blood pressure without causing hypokalemia.',
  'Potassium-Sparing Diuretics - ENaC Blockers (e.g., Amiloride, Triamterene): Target: epithelial sodium channels (ENaCs) in the collecting duct (principal cells). Mechanism: directly inhibits ENaCs, reducing sodium reabsorption and potassium secretion. Effects: causes mild diuresis and prevents potassium loss (potassium-sparing effect).'
],

// **LO-4** (Topic-57) CRITICAL
// Characterize the renal tubular segments based on their water permeability. Identify the tubular segment where water permeability is under hormonal control.
officialDefinitions: [
  'Proximal Tubule - Water Permeability: High. Water is reabsorbed by both transcellular and paracellular pathways. Transcellular reabsorption is mediated by aquaporin-1 (AQP1) channels located in both apical and basolateral membranes. Paracellular reabsorption occurs through tight junctions driven by osmotic gradients. Water reabsorption is isosmotic (~300 mOsm/L). Reabsorbs ~65–70% of filtered water.',
  'Descending Limb of Loop of Henle - Water Permeability: High. Highly permeable to water but impermeable to solutes. Mediated by AQP1 channels. Water is drawn out into the hyperosmotic medullary interstitium. Concentrates tubular fluid to ~1200 mOsm/L at the medullary tip.',
  'Thick Ascending Limb of Loop of Henle - Water Permeability: Very Low (Impermeable). Lacks aquaporins, making it impermeable to water. Na⁺, K⁺, and Cl⁻ are actively reabsorbed via NKCC2, while water remains in lumen. Known as the "diluting segment." Reduces tubular fluid osmolality to ~100 mOsm/L.',
  'Distal Convoluted Tubule - Water Permeability: Very Low (Impermeable). Lacks aquaporins and is impermeable to water under normal conditions. Na⁺ and Cl⁻ are reabsorbed via NCC symporter. Further dilutes tubular fluid to ~50-75 mOsm/L.',
  'Collecting Duct - Water Permeability: Variable (HORMONAL CONTROL). The collecting duct is the only tubular segment where water permeability is under hormonal control via antidiuretic hormone (ADH). Without ADH: water permeability is low, aquaporin-2 (AQP2) channels are absent from apical membrane, tubular fluid remains dilute, producing dilute urine (~50–100 mOsm/L). With ADH: ADH binds to V2 receptors on basolateral membrane of principal cells, activates cAMP signaling cascade leading to insertion of AQP2 channels into apical membrane, water is reabsorbed into hyperosmotic medulla, concentrating urine (~1200 mOsm/L). AQP3 and AQP4 channels are always present on basolateral membrane.'
],

// **LO-5** (Topic-57)
// Define glomerulotubular balance. Describe the proximal tubular reabsorption responsible for glomerulotubular balance.
officialDefinitions: [
  'Glomerulotubular balance: the intrinsic mechanism by which the proximal tubule adjusts its reabsorption of water and solutes to match the filtered load from the glomerulus. This ensures a constant proportion (~65–70%) of the glomerular filtrate is reabsorbed, regardless of changes in the glomerular filtration rate (GFR).',
  'Purpose of glomerulotubular balance: (1) Prevents excessive loss of fluid and solutes in the urine when GFR increases. (2) Maintains a balance between filtration and reabsorption to stabilize extracellular fluid volume. (3) Acts as a second line of defense after the autoregulation of GFR to maintain homeostasis.',
  'Mechanism - Peritubular Capillary Starling Forces: More filtration leads to more plasma water lost while proteins stay behind. This raises the oncotic pressure (π) in peritubular capillaries. At the same time, hydrostatic pressure is low. This creates a strong suction force, pulling fluid back from the tubule into the capillaries.',
  'Mechanism - Proximal Tubule Response: Brush border microvilli detect faster flow and higher Na⁺ delivery. This activates Na⁺-linked transporters like NHE3 (Na⁺/H⁺ exchanger) and SGLT (Na⁺-glucose transporters). Na⁺ reabsorption increases and water follows by osmosis. As solutes are reabsorbed, water follows, which raises the concentration of other solutes (e.g., Cl⁻, urea) in the tubule leading to their passive reabsorption.',
  'Net Result: Even though GFR changes, the proximal tubule reabsorbs the same percentage (fraction) of the filtered load. More filtered leads to more reabsorbed; less filtered leads to less reabsorbed.'
],

// **LO-6** (Topic-57) CRITICAL
// Describe the changes of osmolarity in the tubular fluid and in the interstitial fluid from the loop of Henle, and its importance for the dilution and concentration of urine.
officialDefinitions: [
  'Descending Limb of Loop of Henle - Tubular Fluid: Osmolarity increases as tubular fluid descends into the medulla. Water is reabsorbed (due to high interstitial osmolarity), but solutes (Na⁺, Cl⁻) remain in the lumen, concentrating the tubular fluid. Osmolarity increases from ~300 mOsm/L at the start of the loop to ~1200 mOsm/L at the tip of the loop (in the inner medulla).',
  'Descending Limb - Interstitial Fluid: The interstitial fluid in the medulla has high osmolarity due to active reabsorption of Na⁺, K⁺, and Cl⁻ in the thick ascending limb, and urea recycling from the collecting ducts. Interstitial osmolarity increases progressively from the cortex to the inner medulla, reaching ~1200 mOsm/L.',
  'Thin Ascending Limb - Tubular Fluid: Osmolarity decreases as tubular fluid ascends. This segment is impermeable to water, so water remains in the lumen while some solutes (Na⁺, Cl⁻) diffuse passively into the interstitium. Osmolarity decreases from ~1200 mOsm/L to ~400–500 mOsm/L.',
  'Thick Ascending Limb - Tubular Fluid: Osmolarity continues to decrease, reaching ~100 mOsm/L by the time it leaves the loop of Henle. This segment is impermeable to water but actively reabsorbs Na⁺, K⁺, and Cl⁻ via NKCC2. The removal of solutes without water dilutes the tubular fluid, earning this segment the name "diluting segment."',
  'Importance for Urine Dilution: Occurs in the thick ascending limb and distal convoluted tubule. Water remains in the tubular lumen while solutes are reabsorbed, producing hypotonic tubular fluid (~100 mOsm/L). In the absence of ADH, the collecting duct remains impermeable to water, and dilute urine is excreted (50–100 mOsm/L).',
  'Importance for Urine Concentration: Occurs in the collecting ducts, which reabsorb water in the presence of ADH. The high osmolarity of the medullary interstitium (created by the loop of Henle and urea recycling) provides the osmotic gradient necessary for water reabsorption. Tubular fluid osmolarity increases, producing concentrated urine (up to 1200 mOsm/L).'
],

// **LO-7** (Topic-57) CRITICAL
// Explain the countercurrent multiplier mechanism of the loop of Henle. Contrast NaCl reabsorption in thick vs thin ascending limb. Explain the role of urea.
officialDefinitions: [
  'Countercurrent multiplier mechanism: a process that creates and maintains the medullary osmotic gradient, allowing the kidney to concentrate or dilute urine. Depends on the unique structural arrangement of the descending limb and ascending limb, which have opposite flow directions (countercurrent) and distinct permeability and transport characteristics.',
  'Role of Countercurrent Design: The loop of Henle consists of two limbs with tubular fluid flowing in opposite directions: Descending Limb (fluid flows down into the medulla) and Ascending Limb (fluid flows up toward the cortex). This countercurrent flow enhances the efficiency of solute and water exchange, maximizing the medullary osmotic gradient. The active transport of solutes in the thick ascending limb "multiplies" the osmotic gradient along the length of the loop, ensuring a progressive increase in osmolarity from renal cortex (~300 mOsm/L) to inner medulla (~1200 mOsm/L).',
  'Descending Limb Transport: Highly permeable to water due to aquaporin-1 (AQP1) channels. Impermeable to solutes. Water is reabsorbed into hyperosmotic interstitium by osmosis. Tubular fluid becomes progressively concentrated, reaching ~1200 mOsm/L at the tip of the loop. Function: concentrates tubular fluid by removing water.',
  'Thin Ascending Limb (long-loop juxtamedullary nephrons): Impermeable to water. Permits PASSIVE diffusion of NaCl into the interstitium. Solutes (NaCl) move down their concentration gradient from the tubular fluid into the medullary interstitium. NO active transport occurs in this segment. Function: helps maintain the medullary osmotic gradient by adding solutes to the interstitium.',
  'Thick Ascending Limb: Impermeable to water. ACTIVE reabsorption of Na⁺, K⁺, and Cl⁻ via the Na⁺–K⁺–2Cl⁻ symporter (NKCC2). The sodium gradient is maintained by the Na⁺–K⁺ ATPase pump on the basolateral membrane. Function: dilutes tubular fluid as solutes are removed without water following; contributes to hyperosmotic medullary interstitium.',
  'Role of Urea - Urea Recycling: Urea contributes to ~50% of the medullary osmolarity in the inner medulla (~600 mOsm/L out of 1200 mOsm/L). Urea is passively reabsorbed in the inner medullary collecting duct (regulated by ADH). Reabsorbed urea enters the medullary interstitium and diffuses into the thin descending and ascending limbs of the loop of Henle. Urea is "recycled," maintaining high urea concentrations in the inner medulla. Function: enhances the osmotic gradient in the inner medulla, promoting water reabsorption in the presence of ADH; plays a critical role in urine concentration.'
],

// **LO-8** (Topic-57) CRITICAL
// Describe the role of the countercurrent organization of renal medullary blood flow through the vasa recta on retaining the medullary osmotic gradient (countercurrent exchanger).
officialDefinitions: [
  'Vasa recta: the capillary network surrounding the loop of Henle in the renal medulla, plays a crucial role in preserving the medullary osmotic gradient through a process called the countercurrent exchanger. This mechanism allows the vasa recta to deliver nutrients and oxygen to the medulla while minimizing the disruption of the osmotic gradient necessary for urine concentration.',
  'Countercurrent Organization: The vasa recta consists of long, thin capillaries that run parallel to the loops of Henle in a U-shaped arrangement. Blood flow in the vasa recta is countercurrent: blood flows downward into the medulla in the descending portion of the vasa recta, and blood flows upward toward the cortex in the ascending portion.',
  'Descending Vasa Recta: As blood flows down into the medulla, water diffuses out of the descending vasa recta into the hyperosmotic medullary interstitium (driven by the medullary osmotic gradient). Solutes (Na⁺, Cl⁻, urea) diffuse into the descending vasa recta, increasing the osmolarity of the blood as it approaches the inner medulla.',
  'Ascending Vasa Recta: As blood flows upward from the medulla toward the cortex, water diffuses into the ascending vasa recta (drawn by the lower osmolarity of the interstitium in the outer medulla and cortex). Solutes (Na⁺, Cl⁻, urea) diffuse out of the ascending vasa recta back into the medullary interstitium.',
  'Net Effect: The U-shaped design ensures that solutes picked up by the descending vasa recta are returned to the interstitium by the ascending vasa recta, and water removed from the interstitium by the descending vasa recta is returned to the bloodstream by the ascending vasa recta. This countercurrent exchange prevents the "washout" of the medullary osmotic gradient.',
  'Features Supporting Gradient Preservation: (1) Low Blood Flow: medullary blood flow in the vasa recta is low (~1–2% of renal blood flow), reducing the potential for solute washout. (2) Permeability: the walls of the vasa recta are permeable to both water and solutes, enabling passive diffusion in both directions. (3) U-Shaped Design: ensures solutes and water are exchanged without disrupting the medullary osmotic gradient.',
  'Importance: Preserves the osmotic gradient established by the countercurrent multiplier mechanism in the loop of Henle. Essential for water reabsorption in the collecting duct (allowing production of concentrated urine in presence of ADH). Enables efficient reabsorption of urea (urea recycling contributes to osmotic gradient in inner medulla).'
],
