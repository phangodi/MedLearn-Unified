// **LO-1** (Topic-31)
officialDefinitions: [
  'Central chemoreceptors: located in the brain stem, are the most important for the minute-to-minute control of breathing. These chemoreceptors are located on the ventral surface of the medulla, near the point of exit of the glossopharyngeal (CN IX) and vagus (CN X) nerves and only a short distance from the DRG in the medulla. Thus central chemoreceptors communicate directly with the inspiratory center.',
  'Peripheral chemoreceptors: located in the carotid bodies at the bifurcation of the common carotid arteries and in the aortic bodies above and below the aortic arch. Information about arterial PO2, PCO2, and pH is relayed to the DRG via CN IX and CN X, which orchestrates an appropriate change in breathing rate.',
  'Importance for detecting changes in blood gases: Central chemoreceptors respond to changes in pCO2 and are exquisitely sensitive to changes in the pH of cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). Even a small increase (2-5 mmHg) in pCO2 can lead to a significant increase in ventilation. Decreases in the pH of CSF produce increases in breathing rate (hyperventilation), and increases in the pH of CSF produce decreases in breathing rate (hypoventilation).',
  'Peripheral chemoreceptors detect changes in arterial pO2, pCO2, and pH. Hypoxemia only becomes a strong ventilatory driver when pO2 falls below the threshold of approximately 60 mmHg. While pO2 affects ventilation, its influence is less significant than that of pCO2 under normal conditions. Peripheral chemoreceptors contribute 30% to CO2 response (70% affect the central chemoreceptor).',
  'Glomus Caroticum (Carotid Body): Sensitive to changes in both arterial pH (pHa) and partial pressure of carbon dioxide (PaCO2). These receptors respond to a decrease in pH (increased acidity) and an increase in PaCO2 (hypercapnia) by increasing the respiratory drive, aiming to balance blood gas levels and pH through faster and deeper breathing.',
  'Glomus Aorticum (Aortic Body): Unlike the carotid body, the aortic body does not respond to changes in pH. It primarily responds to changes in oxygen levels (PaO2) rather than pH or PaCO2. This means the aortic body is more focused on detecting oxygen deficiency (hypoxia) rather than changes in blood acidity or carbon dioxide levels.'

The protons will inhibit potassium channel.',

// **LO-3** (Topic-31)
officialDefinitions: [
  'Location: Central chemoreceptors are dorsally and laterally in aspect of the medulla, in the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF).',
  'Function: Control blood pH in response to changing and fluctuating pCO2 levels. Sense any changes in the pH in cerebral spinal fluid and some of the interstitial fluid in the Central Nervous System (CNS). When CO2 levels rise, carbonic acid, protons and bicarbonate can stimulate the central chemoreceptors. When CO2 levels drop (hypocapnia), it decreases carbonic acid, protons and will inhibit the center.',
  'Response to pCO2: The medullary chemoreceptors respond directly to changes in the pH of CSF and indirectly to changes in arterial PCO2. In the blood, CO2 combines reversibly with H2O to form H+ and HCO3− by the familiar reactions. Because the blood-brain barrier is relatively impermeable to H+ and HCO3−, these ions are trapped in the vascular compartment and do not enter the brain. CO2, however, is quite permeable across the blood-brain barrier and enters the extracellular fluid of the brain.',
  'Mechanism in CSF: CO2 also is permeable across the brain-CSF barrier and enters the CSF. In the CSF, CO2 is converted to H+ and HCO3−. Thus increases in arterial PCO2 produce increases in the PCO2 of CSF, which results in an increase in H+ concentration of CSF (decrease in pH). The central chemoreceptors are in close proximity to CSF and detect the decrease in pH. A decrease in pH then signals the inspiratory center to increase the breathing rate (hyperventilation).',
  'Goal: The goal of central chemoreceptors is to keep arterial PCO2 within the normal range, if possible. Thus increases in arterial PCO2 produce increases in PCO2 in the brain and the CSF, which decreases the pH of the CSF. A decrease in CSF pH is detected by central chemoreceptors for H+, which instruct the DRG to increase the breathing rate. When the breathing rate increases, more CO2 will be expired and the arterial PCO2 will decrease toward normal.',
  'Note: CO2 is the most powerful respiratory stimulus, with regards the oxygen level is within the normal. When oxygen levels drop < 60 mmHg, it becomes the most powerful. Protons cannot directly pass the blood brain barrier into the CSF because they are charged.'

// **LO-4** (Topic-31)
officialDefinitions: [
  'Changes in pCO2 (Primary Driver) - Increase in pCO2 (Hypercapnia): When pCO2 levels in the blood rise, central chemoreceptors in the medulla oblongata detect an increase in hydrogen ion concentration (resulting from the formation of carbonic acid). This leads to increased alveolar ventilation (hyperventilation) to expel more CO2, thereby reducing blood pCO2 and restoring pH to normal levels.',
  'Changes in pCO2 - Decrease in pCO2 (Hypocapnia): When pCO2 levels drop, ventilation is decreased (hypoventilation) to allow CO2 to accumulate in the blood, preventing alkalosis (an increase in pH).',
  'Sensitivity to pCO2: Central chemoreceptors are highly sensitive to changes in pCO2. Even a small increase (2-5 mmHg) can lead to a significant increase in ventilation.',
  'Changes in pO2 - Decrease in pO2 (Hypoxemia): Peripheral chemoreceptors (carotid and aortic bodies) are sensitive to low blood oxygen levels. When pO2 drops significantly (below 60 mmHg), these chemoreceptors stimulate the respiratory centers to increase ventilation.',
  'Changes in pO2 - Role Compared to pCO2: While pO2 affects ventilation, its influence is less significant than that of pCO2 under normal conditions. Hypoxemia only becomes a strong ventilatory driver when pO2 falls below the threshold of approximately 60 mmHg.',
  'Changes in pO2 - Increase in pO2: Higher levels of pO2 do not significantly change alveolar ventilation, as normal levels of pO2 are already sufficient for oxygen saturation.',
  'Combined Changes - Low pO2 and High pCO2: This combination synergistically increases alveolar ventilation. The body\'s response to hypoxemia is amplified when it occurs alongside hypercapnia, triggering an even stronger ventilatory response to expel CO2 and take in more O2.',
  'Combined Changes - High pO2 and High pCO2: High pO2 does not counteract the ventilatory drive induced by high pCO2. Central chemoreceptors will still increase ventilation in response to hypercapnia.',
  'Combined Changes - Low pCO2 and Low pO2: If pCO2 is low (hypocapnia), the drive to ventilate decreases. Even if pO2 is low, the hypoventilatory effect of low pCO2 may blunt the body\'s hypoxic ventilatory response.',
  'CO2 Narcosis (Hypercapnic Narcosis): CO2 narcosis is a condition characterized by depressed central nervous system function due to an excessively high level of CO2 in the blood (severe hypercapnia).',
  'CO2 Narcosis - Mechanism: High levels of pCO2 (e.g., >70-80 mmHg) can cross the blood-brain barrier and increase the concentration of hydrogen ions in the cerebrospinal fluid, resulting in central nervous system depression.',
  'CO2 Narcosis - Symptoms: Early signs include headache, confusion, and drowsiness. As CO2 levels increase further, more severe symptoms such as reduced consciousness, lethargy, and potentially coma can occur.',
  'CO2 Narcosis - Clinical Relevance: CO2 narcosis is often seen in patients with chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD) who may have impaired ventilation and a decreased response to hypercapnia over time. These patients rely more on their hypoxic drive for ventilation, and supplemental oxygen therapy needs to be carefully managed to prevent suppression of this drive.'
],

// **LO-5** (Topic-31)
officialDefinitions: [
  'Normal physiology: In normal physiology, breathing is primarily driven by central chemoreceptors located in the medulla oblongata. These receptors respond to elevated CO₂ levels (through changes in cerebrospinal fluid pH) to increase ventilation.',
  'Central Chemoreceptor Adaptation in Chronic Hypercapnia (e.g., COPD): In chronic respiratory diseases like chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), CO₂ levels are chronically elevated. Over time, the central chemoreceptors adapt (become less sensitive) to high CO₂ and no longer stimulate ventilation effectively.',
  'Shift to Hypoxic Respiratory Drive: As a result, the body shifts to rely more on peripheral chemoreceptors in the carotid and aortic bodies, which respond to low oxygen levels (hypoxemia). This is called "hypoxic respiratory drive."',
  'Driving force for respiration: In chronic increase of the arterial PCO2 or sleeping pill poisoning, the driving force for the respiration becomes O2 receptors.',
  'Consequence of Administering Supplemental Oxygen: If high concentrations of oxygen are administered to these patients, peripheral chemoreceptor activity decreases (because PaO₂ rises). This reduces the respiratory drive, leading to hypoventilation, further CO₂ retention (worsening hypercapnia), and respiratory acidosis.',
  'Severe consequences: In severe cases, loss of consciousness or respiratory arrest may occur.'

// **LO-6** (Topic-31)
officialDefinitions: [
  'Immediate Changes in Alveolar Ventilation After Traveling to High Elevation - Reduced Oxygen Availability: At high elevation, the atmospheric pressure is lower, leading to a decrease in the partial pressure of oxygen (pO2) in the air and subsequently in the alveoli (PAO2).',
  'Immediate Changes - Hypoxic Drive Activation: The lower PAO2 stimulates the peripheral chemoreceptors (located in the carotid and aortic bodies), which respond to the drop in arterial pO2 (PaO2) by signaling the respiratory center in the medulla to increase alveolar ventilation.',
  'Immediate Changes - Increased Ventilation: As a result, there is an immediate increase in the rate and depth of breathing (hyperventilation) to improve oxygen uptake and increase PAO2.',
  'Immediate Changes - CO2 Levels: The increase in ventilation also leads to more CO2 being expelled, which lowers the arterial partial pressure of CO2 (PaCO2), potentially causing respiratory alkalosis due to a decrease in blood H⁺ concentration.',
  'Changes After 2 Weeks of Acclimation - Continued Hyperventilation: The body continues to hyperventilate to maintain sufficient oxygen levels. However, over time, several compensatory mechanisms develop.',
  'Acclimation - Renal Compensation (Bicarbonate Excretion): The kidneys compensate for the respiratory alkalosis by increasing the excretion of bicarbonate (HCO₃⁻), which helps to normalize blood pH levels. This allows the central chemoreceptors to become more tolerant of the lower PaCO2 and sustain increased ventilation.',
  'Acclimation - Increased Red Blood Cell Production (Erythropoiesis): The hypoxic conditions stimulate the production of erythropoietin (EPO) by the kidneys, leading to an increase in red blood cell (RBC) production and, consequently, a higher hemoglobin concentration to enhance oxygen-carrying capacity.',
  'Acclimation - Increased 2,3-Bisphosphoglycerate (2,3-BPG): Right Shift of the Oxyhemoglobin Dissociation Curve. The concentration of 2,3-BPG in RBCs increases, facilitating the release of oxygen to the tissues by decreasing hemoglobin\'s affinity for oxygen.',
  'Acclimation - Ventilatory Adaptation (Sensitivity of Chemoreceptors): The peripheral chemoreceptors become more sensitive to low oxygen levels over time, maintaining the drive for increased ventilation even as renal compensation normalizes the pH. Changes in Respiratory Patterns: The body may also adapt its breathing pattern to optimize the balance between oxygen uptake and carbon dioxide elimination.',
  'Immediate Changes After Returning to Sea Level - Sudden Increase in Atmospheric Pressure: Returning to sea level means an increase in atmospheric pressure and a higher pO2 in inspired air.',
  'Returning to Sea Level - Reduced Hypoxic Drive: The increased pO2 reduces stimulation of the peripheral chemoreceptors, leading to a decrease in the hypoxic drive for increased ventilation.',
  'Returning to Sea Level - Decreased Alveolar Ventilation (Return to Baseline): Alveolar ventilation begins to return to normal as the body no longer needs to compensate for low oxygen availability. This may be slower due to adaptations that occurred at high altitude, such as the increased RBC count and 2,3-BPG levels. The body may initially experience a state of mild hyperoxia as it adjusts back to sea level conditions.',
  'Returning to Sea Level - CO2 Levels (Rebound Increase in PaCO2): With decreased ventilation, PaCO2 may temporarily increase until chemoreceptors adjust and help stabilize ventilation to maintain normal pCO2 levels.'
],

// **LO-7** (Topic-31)
officialDefinitions: [
  'Feed-forward control of ventilation: refers to the anticipatory mechanism by which the respiratory system increases the rate and depth of breathing in preparation for or at the onset of increased physical activity. Unlike feedback mechanisms, which respond after a change has occurred (e.g., when blood CO2 or O2 levels are detected as altered), feed-forward control proactively adjusts ventilation before significant changes in blood gas concentrations can take place. This type of control allows the body to maintain homeostasis during periods of increased metabolic demand, such as exercise.',
  'Importance - Anticipatory Response: Feed-forward control involves the nervous system preemptively increasing the rate and depth of breathing in response to signals from the motor cortex and proprioceptors in the muscles and joints. This prepares the respiratory system for the increased oxygen demand and carbon dioxide production that occurs during exercise.',
  'Importance - Rapid Adjustment: By increasing ventilation promptly at the onset of exercise, feed-forward control prevents significant changes in blood gas concentrations, maintaining homeostasis.',
  'Importance - Prevention of Hypoxia and Hypercapnia: The increased ventilation ensures that oxygen delivery meets the demands of the tissues and that carbon dioxide, a byproduct of metabolism, is effectively removed from the body.',
  'Effect on Arterial pCO2 (Maintained or Slightly Reduced): During steady-state exercise, the increased ventilation effectively removes CO2 from the body at a rate that matches its production. This prevents a rise in arterial pCO2, and in some cases, it may slightly decrease due to hyperventilation associated with intense exercise.',
  'Effect on Arterial pO2 (Maintained): Despite the increased oxygen consumption by muscles, arterial pO2 typically remains stable due to the increased rate and depth of breathing. This ensures that the oxygen levels in the blood remain adequate for muscle function.',
  'Effect on Arterial pH (Slight Decrease During Intense Exercise): At the onset of moderate exercise, pH usually remains stable. However, during high-intensity exercise, lactate production can lead to a temporary decrease in pH (metabolic acidosis). The body compensates through increased ventilation to remove CO2 (which forms carbonic acid in the blood), helping to buffer the drop in pH.',
  'Integration: Proprioceptors are found within the tendons, muscle spindles, Golgi tendon organs, and joint capsules. Stimulated proprioceptors send impulse to the CNS through the dorsal root ganglion. The medial lemnisci stimulate DRG, which sends impulses to VRG, increasing action potentials to the phrenic and intercostal nerves, stimulating intercostals to contract and depress the diaphragm, increasing respiration rate and depth.'
