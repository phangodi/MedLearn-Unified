officialDefinitions: [
  'Regional Distribution: In a standing human, pulmonary blood flow is unevenly distributed due to gravity. Blood flow is highest in the base of the lungs and lowest at the apex.',
  'Pulmonary Zones of Blood Flow: The lung can be divided into three zones (West zones) based on the relationship between pulmonary arterial pressure (P_a), pulmonary venous pressure (P_v), and alveolar pressure (P_A).',
  'Zone I: Apex of the Lung - Location: Uppermost part of the lung. Pressure Relationships: PA > Pa > Pv (Alveolar pressure exceeds arterial pressure, which exceeds venous pressure). Effect on Blood Flow: The high alveolar pressure compresses the pulmonary capillaries, preventing blood flow. This zone is physiologically absent in healthy individuals at normal cardiac output. However, it may occur under conditions like hypotension or positive pressure ventilation. Blood Flow: Minimal or absent.',
  'Zone II: Mid-Lung - Location: Middle region of the lung. Pressure Relationships: Pa > PA > Pv (Arterial pressure exceeds alveolar pressure, which exceeds venous pressure). Effect on Blood Flow: Blood flow occurs, but it is determined by the difference between arterial and alveolar pressures (P_a - P_A). Venous pressure does not influence flow because it is less than alveolar pressure. Blood Flow: Moderate.',
  'Zone III: Base of the Lung - Location: Lowest part of the lung. Pressure Relationships: Pa > Pv > PA (Arterial and venous pressures both exceed alveolar pressure). Effect on Blood Flow: Blood flow is determined by the difference between arterial and venous pressures (Pa - Pv). Pulmonary capillaries are fully perfused. Blood Flow: Maximum.',
  'Key Factors Influencing Blood Flow Distribution: Gravity: Causes higher hydrostatic pressure in the lower regions of the lungs, resulting in more blood flow at the base. Pulmonary Vascular Resistance: Varies across the zones due to the interaction of alveolar and vascular pressures. Exercise: Increases cardiac output, raising pulmonary arterial pressure, which may convert zone I regions into zone II or zone III regions. Hypovolemia: Reduces pulmonary arterial and venous pressures, potentially enlarging zone I. Positive Pressure Ventilation: Increases alveolar pressure, potentially converting zone II or III regions into zone I.'

// **LO-3** (Topic-50)
officialDefinitions: [
  'Pulmonary Vascular Resistance (PVR): Pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR) is inversely related to increases in cardiac output (CO) and pulmonary arterial pressure (PAP). This adaptive mechanism ensures that the pulmonary circulation accommodates changes in blood flow during various physiological states, such as exercise, while maintaining efficient gas exchange and protecting the right ventricle from excessive pressure.',
  'Relationship Between Cardiac Output, Pulmonary Arterial Pressure, and PVR: Increase in Cardiac Output: Increased CO raises blood flow in the pulmonary circulation. Instead of causing a proportional rise in PAP, PVR decreases to accommodate the additional flow. Increase in Pulmonary Arterial Pressure: As PAP increases, PVR decreases due to mechanisms that increase the vascular bed\'s capacity, ensuring minimal resistance to flow.',
  'Capillary Recruitment: At rest, many pulmonary capillaries are collapsed or minimally perfused. When CO or PAP increases, these capillaries open up (recruitment), allowing blood to flow through previously unperfused regions. Recruitment primarily occurs in the mid and upper lung regions, where perfusion is normally limited due to gravitational effects. Recruitment increases the total cross-sectional area of the pulmonary vascular bed, significantly reducing resistance.',
  'Vascular Distension: Pulmonary capillaries are highly compliant and can expand (distend) in response to increased pressure. Distension reduces the resistance in already perfused capillaries, further decreasing overall PVR. Distension is particularly important in maintaining low resistance at higher blood flows.',
  'Combined Effects of Recruitment and Distension: At low cardiac output or pulmonary arterial pressure: Few capillaries are open, and those that are open are minimally distended. PVR is relatively high. As cardiac output or pressure increases: Recruitment and distension progressively reduce PVR, preventing a significant rise in PAP. This maintains efficient gas exchange and minimizes the workload on the right heart.'
],

officialDefinitions: [
  'Pulmonary Vascular Resistance and Lung Volume: Pulmonary vascular resistance (PVR) changes dynamically with lung volume due to the differential behavior of two main types of pulmonary vessels: Alveolar vessels: Small capillaries embedded within the alveolar walls. Extraalveolar vessels: Larger arteries and veins that lie outside the alveoli and are influenced by surrounding lung parenchyma.',
  'U-shaped Relationship: The relationship between lung volume and PVR follows a U-shaped curve: At very low lung volumes: PVR is high. At functional residual capacity (FRC): PVR is at its minimum. At very high lung volumes: PVR increases again.',
  'Alveolar Vessels: Location: These vessels are surrounded by alveoli. At low lung volumes: Less compression → capillaries are more open → ↓ resistance. At high lung volumes: Alveolar expansion compresses the alveolar capillaries, increasing resistance due to vessel narrowing. Effect: PVR increases as lung volume moves away from FRC in either direction.',
  'Extraalveolar Vessels: Location: These vessels lie outside the alveoli and are surrounded by the lung parenchyma. At low lung volumes: The lung parenchyma provides little radial traction, causing the extraalveolar vessels to collapse, increasing PVR. At high lung volumes: Increased lung expansion stretches the parenchyma, exerting radial traction that dilates the extraalveolar vessels and decreases resistance. Effect: PVR decreases as lung volume increases.',
  'Why PVR Is Lowest at FRC: At FRC, the opposing effects on alveolar and extraalveolar vessels balance out, producing minimal overall resistance. Moving away from FRC in either direction increases total PVR.'
],

// **LO-5** (Topic-50) - CRITICAL
officialDefinitions: [
  'Pulmonary Hypoxic Vasoconstriction (HPV): Pulmonary hypoxic vasoconstriction (HPV), or the Euler-Liljestrand reflex, is a physiological mechanism in which pulmonary arterioles constrict in response to a decrease in alveolar oxygen partial pressure (PaO₂). This response redirects blood flow from poorly ventilated, hypoxic regions of the lung to better-ventilated regions, improving ventilation-perfusion (V/Q) matching and gas exchange efficiency.',
  'The Euler–Liljestrand Reflex: The Euler–Liljestrand reflex is a local, intrinsic pulmonary response in which hypoxia (low alveolar O₂) causes vasoconstriction of pulmonary arterioles that supply poorly ventilated alveoli. Purpose: To optimize ventilation-perfusion (V/Q) matching by diverting blood away from hypoxic (poorly ventilated) regions of the lung and toward well-oxygenated areas. This improves overall gas exchange efficiency.',
  'Mechanism - Trigger: Hypoxia (Low Alveolar PaO₂): Alveolar oxygen partial pressure below 70 mmHg is the threshold for initiating vasoconstriction in the surrounding pulmonary arterioles.',
  'Mechanism - Local Pulmonary Response: Pulmonary arteriolar smooth muscle cells in hypoxic regions contract. This constriction reduces blood flow (perfusion) to the poorly ventilated alveoli and redistributes it to better-ventilated alveoli.',
  'Mechanism - Molecular Mechanism: Oxygen Sensing: Hypoxia inhibits oxygen-sensitive potassium (K⁺) channels in the pulmonary arterial smooth muscle cells. Membrane Depolarization: Reduced K⁺ efflux leads to depolarization of the smooth muscle cell membrane. Calcium Influx: Depolarization opens voltage-gated calcium channels, allowing an influx of calcium ions (Ca²⁺). Smooth Muscle Contraction: Increased intracellular calcium activates contractile proteins, causing vasoconstriction.',
  'Role in Blood Flow Regulation: Ventilation-Perfusion Matching: Primary Function: HPV improves V/Q matching by redirecting blood flow to well-ventilated alveoli. Efficiency: This minimizes perfusion to hypoxic alveoli where oxygen uptake would be inefficient. Adaptation to Localized Hypoxia: If only specific regions of the lung are poorly ventilated (e.g., due to pneumonia or airway obstruction), HPV limits perfusion to these regions, preserving overall gas exchange.',
  'Pathological Implications: Generalized Hypoxia: In conditions like chronic obstructive pulmonary disease (COPD), high-altitude hypoxia, or severe asthma, widespread HPV can lead to pulmonary hypertension. High-Altitude Pulmonary Edema (HAPE): At high altitudes, excessive HPV increases pulmonary artery pressure, potentially causing fluid leakage into the alveoli.'
],

officialDefinitions: [
  'Nitric Oxide (NO): Nitric oxide (NO) is a potent endogenous vasodilator that primarily affects vascular smooth muscle cells. When inhaled, it has selective effects on the pulmonary circulation due to its short half-life and localised action.',
  'Mechanism of Action of Inhaled NO: Vascular Target: Inhaled NO diffuses across the alveolar-capillary membrane and acts on pulmonary vascular smooth muscle cells near well-ventilated alveoli. Signal Transduction: NO activates soluble guanylate cyclase (sGC) in smooth muscle cells. This increases the intracellular concentration of cyclic GMP (cGMP). Elevated cGMP causes relaxation of smooth muscle cells, leading to vasodilation. Selective Action: Because NO is rapidly inactivated by haemoglobin in the blood, its effects are limited to the pulmonary circulation. This minimizes systemic vasodilation.',
  'Effect on Hypoxic Vasoconstriction (HPV): Reduction of Local Vasoconstriction: Hypoxic pulmonary vasoconstriction (HPV) is a compensatory mechanism that redirects blood flow from poorly ventilated (hypoxic) alveoli to better-ventilated regions. Inhaled NO selectively dilates pulmonary vessels in ventilated regions, counteracting HPV. Improved Ventilation-Perfusion Matching: Inhaled NO enhances blood flow to well-ventilated alveoli, optimizing oxygenation. This reduces intrapulmonary shunting and improves arterial oxygenation.',
  'Effect on Pulmonary Vascular Resistance (PVR): Decreased PVR in Ventilated Regions: NO lowers PVR in well-ventilated lung areas by relaxing pulmonary arterioles. This can alleviate pulmonary hypertension caused by increased PVR. Selective Action Preserves HPV in Non-Ventilated Regions: NO does not affect non-ventilated regions because it cannot diffuse to poorly ventilated alveoli. This selective action helps preserve the beneficial effects of HPV in directing blood away from hypoxic regions.'

  'Bronchial Circulation: The bronchial circulation is a part of the systemic circulation that supplies oxygenated blood to the airways and supporting structures of the lungs. Unlike the pulmonary circulation, which primarily participates in gas exchange, the bronchial circulation serves the metabolic needs of lung tissue.',
  'Origin of Bronchial Arteries: The bronchial arteries originate from the systemic circulation, usually arising from: The descending thoracic aorta (most common). Occasionally, from intercostal arteries or the subclavian arteries.',
  'Distribution: Supplies the conducting airways down to the level of terminal bronchioles. Provides blood to the visceral pleura, large pulmonary vessels, and hilar lymph nodes.',
  'Venous Drainage: Blood from the bronchial veins drains into two systems: Bronchial veins, which empty into the azygos and hemiazygos systems and eventually into the superior vena cava. Pulmonary veins, where some bronchial venous blood is shunted and contributes to the normal physiological shunt.',
  'Low Flow, High Pressure: Compared to pulmonary circulation, the bronchial circulation has: Low flow: It receives only about 1% of the cardiac output. High pressure: As part of the systemic circulation, it operates under higher pressures than the pulmonary circulation.',
  'Anastomoses with Pulmonary Circulation: Bronchial and pulmonary circulations are interconnected via anastomoses. These anastomoses ensure perfusion to lung tissue even if one of the circulations is compromised.',
  'Functions of the Bronchial Circulation: Nutritional Supply: Provides oxygen and nutrients to: Conducting airways (trachea, bronchi, and bronchioles). Supporting tissues of the lung, including the connective tissue and pleura. Thermoregulation: Helps in maintaining the temperature of inspired air by warming it. Humidification of Inspired Air: Plays a role in the humidification of air passing through the airways. Defense Mechanism: Contributes to immune defense by delivering immune cells and mediators to the airways. Collateral Circulation: During pulmonary artery obstruction (e.g., pulmonary embolism), bronchial circulation can partially maintain perfusion to lung tissue, preventing ischemia.'
