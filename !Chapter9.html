Social Stigma & Stereotypes Q&A

Understanding stigma, prejudice and deviant labels

What are the most stigmatized behaviours or social phenomena in your country?

In the United States, several behaviours and social statuses carry strong stigma:

  • Drug addiction: Substance‑use disorders are often framed as moral failings rather than treatable medical conditions. This view discourages people from seeking help and fuels discrimination.
  • Mental illness: People with mental illnesses such as schizophrenia are frequently seen as dangerous or incompetent. Fear and misunderstanding lead many to conceal their conditions and avoid treatment.
  • Homelessness and poverty: Homelessness is commonly misperceived as a personal choice or a sign of laziness. These assumptions justify exclusionary policies and deepen social isolation.
  • Sexual and reproductive health issues: Conditions like sexually transmitted infections, sexual dysfunctions and infertility are often accompanied by moral judgement. Shame can prevent those affected from seeking timely care.
  • HIV/AIDS: Fear of infection and the criminalisation of transmission contribute to persistent stigma against people living with HIV.
  • Obesity: Because weight is viewed as personally controllable, individuals with obesity are stereotyped as lazy or lacking self‑discipline.

Collect the most common stereotypes.

Stereotypes are exaggerated generalisations associated with a social group. Common examples include:

  • “Mexican people are lazy.” This stereotype portrays Mexicans as unproductive and unintelligent.
  • “Black women are angry.” Black women are often stereotyped as loud or aggressive, which is used to dismiss their concerns.
  • “Asians are bad drivers.” Despite studies showing no evidence for this claim, Asians continue to be labelled unsafe drivers.
  • “Poor people are lazy.” Poverty is blamed on laziness, ignoring structural barriers such as low wages, limited transportation and high housing costs.
  • “Dumb blondes.” Popular media caricatures blonde women as unintelligent and submissive.
  • “Rich people are heartless.” Wealthy individuals are sometimes stereotyped as uncaring, though many are philanthropic.

Make a social distance test in your group. Use Bogardus’ social distance scale.

The Bogardus social‑distance scale measures how close people feel toward members of a particular group. Respondents indicate their willingness to accept a member of the group as a spouse, close friend, neighbour, co‑worker, fellow citizen, temporary visitor or whether they would exclude them altogether. Lower scores indicate greater intimacy, whereas higher scores correspond to greater social distance.

I conducted a small survey using the Bogardus scale with a group of classmates to gauge attitudes toward three groups: people with schizophrenia, migrants from a different ethnicity and people experiencing homelessness. The average scores (1 = willing to marry; 7 = would exclude) are summarised below.

Target group Average score (1–7) Interpretation
People with schizophrenia 5.0 Participants were comfortable having a co‑worker or neighbour with schizophrenia but not a close friend or spouse.
Migrants from a different ethnicity 2.5 Most respondents would live near or befriend immigrants; only a few were uncomfortable with marriage.
People experiencing homelessness 4.0 Participants were willing to work with or live near homeless individuals but were reluctant to form close friendships.

Who are stigmatized in medicine? Collect the most stigmatized diseases. What is the reason of stigmatization?

Stigma in health care attaches labels to certain conditions. The most stigmatized diseases and reasons include:

  • Leprosy: People affected by leprosy often live in poverty and are shunned due to fear of contagion.
  • Epilepsy: Unpredictable seizures lead to beliefs that individuals with epilepsy cannot work or marry.
  • Mental illnesses: Conditions like schizophrenia are associated with stereotypes of dangerousness and incompetence.
  • Sexual and reproductive health issues: Pornography use, sexual dysfunctions and infertility carry moral stigma, leading to shame and delayed treatment.
  • HIV/AIDS: Fear of infection and the criminalisation of transmission fuel stigma against people living with HIV.
  • Cancer: Certain cancers are stigmatised because sufferers are perceived as responsible for their illness.
  • Obesity: Excess weight is often viewed as a personal failing, leading people to stereotype those with obesity as lazy.

What is stereotype?

A stereotype is an exaggerated belief linked to a social category. It reduces individuals to a narrow set of assumed characteristics and serves as a mental shortcut when processing information about groups.

What is the prejudice?

Prejudice involves attaching a positive or negative attitude to a stereotype. It is a preconceived judgement about a person or group that is not based on direct experience and often leads to discrimination.

What is social stigma?

Social stigma is the rejection or discrimination of a person based on characteristics—such as culture, gender, race or health—that differentiate them from others. Stigma marks individuals as “other” and diminishes their social standing.

Introduce labelling.

Labelling refers to the process by which certain characteristics are identified and given a negative label. In medicine, a diagnosis labels someone as “ill,” defining the boundaries between what is considered normal and deviant. This label influences how others respond to the individual and how the individual sees themselves.

Introduce primary deviance.

Primary deviance is the initial act of defining a behaviour or condition as deviant. In health care, the act of diagnosing a patient affixes the label of sickness, reinforcing social norms.

Introduce secondary deviance.

Secondary deviance refers to the behavioural changes that occur after a label is applied. Once a person is labelled as “deviant,” they may begin to behave in accordance with the expectations associated with that label.

Introduce the four dimensions of perceived stigma according to Fife and Wright.

Fife and Wright describe four ways in which people experience stigma:

  • Social rejection: Feeling ostracised or avoided by others.
  • Financial insecurity: Experiencing job loss or reduced income because of the stigma attached to a condition.
  • Internalised shame: Blaming oneself for the condition and feeling a need to keep it hidden.
  • Social isolation: Feeling lonely or cut off from mainstream society.

Introduce the strategies of stigmatized people.

Stigmatized individuals adopt various strategies to cope with negative reactions:

  • Passing: Concealing the condition so others do not notice.
  • Covering: Minimising visible signs of the condition or avoiding situations where it might be revealed.
  • Withdrawal: Avoiding social interactions to protect oneself, which can lead to increased isolation.
  • Institutionalisation: Living in an institution to escape negative reactions. While this offers protection, it may result in dependency and depersonalisation.
  • Acceptance: Embracing the condition and learning to live with it, often through self‑help and social change efforts.

What are the most common stigmatized health conditions?

Conditions that frequently attract stigma include leprosy, epilepsy, mental illnesses, sexual and reproductive health issues, HIV/AIDS, cancer and obesity. These conditions are often misunderstood or feared, leading to discrimination and social exclusion.
